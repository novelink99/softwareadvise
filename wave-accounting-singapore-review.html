Wave Accounting Singapore Review

Updated 2026-03-31 · 9 min read · Singapore

Our Verdict

7.8/10

Wave Accounting is a solid, free cloud accounting option for Singapore micro-businesses and sole traders, particularly those just starting out. Strong invoicing, receipt capture, and basic reporting make it compelling at zero cost. However, GST compliance is manual, multi-currency support is limited, and local bank integrations (DBS, OCBC, UOB) are absent—making it less suitable for growing businesses managing complex tax or multi-entity operations. Best for: service providers under SGD 1M revenue with simple bookkeeping needs.

What is Wave Accounting?

Wave is a free cloud-based accounting and invoicing platform operated by Wave Financial Inc. (a subsidiary of H&M Revenue). Unlike traditional accounting software that charges monthly subscriptions, Wave's core product—invoicing, expense tracking, and financial reporting—costs absolutely nothing.

In Singapore, Wave has quietly built a following among freelancers, consultants, and small service businesses that want professional accounting without the SGD 40–150/month price tag of alternatives like Xero or Zoho Books. The platform operates in English and supports SGD as a currency, making it immediately relevant for local operators.

However, Wave monetizes differently: through optional add-ons (payroll, payment processing) and time-tracking features. This model works brilliantly if you need only core accounting, but gaps emerge quickly if you have GST obligations, multiple bank accounts, or growth ambitions.

Key Features for Singapore SMEs

Invoicing & Receipt Capture

Wave's invoicing is genuinely professional and free. You can customize branded invoices, set up recurring billing, and send payment reminders. The receipt capture tool (via mobile app) uses OCR to extract expense data—snap a receipt, and fields auto-populate. For Singapore cash-flow tracking, this is invaluable.

Bank Reconciliation

Wave supports automated reconciliation but with a critical local limitation: it integrates with major UK and US banks (Barclays, Stripe, etc.) and some Canadian institutions, but not with DBS, OCBC, or UOB—Singapore's big three retail banks. You'll reconcile manually, downloading CSV statements and matching transactions by hand. This is tedious at scale.

Multi-Currency & GST Compliance

Wave accepts SGD and MYR (Malaysia Ringgit), but multi-currency transactions aren't seamless—you'll need to manually log FX rates. More problematic: GST tracking is entirely manual. Singapore's 9% GST is not automated; you must tag invoices as GST-registered or mark expenses as GST-claimable, then manually prepare GST returns. For IRAS compliance, this is error-prone and unsuitable if you're GST-registered and file quarterly returns.

Reports & Analytics

Wave generates basic P&L, balance sheets, and cash-flow statements suitable for personal tax filing (ITR) or bank loan applications. You can export to PDF or CSV. For consolidated business insights, reports are functional but not sophisticated—no budget tracking, cash-flow projections, or detailed tax forecasting.

Payments (Stripe) 2.2% + SGD 0.30 per transaction Accept online payments; funds settle in 1–2 days

Wave's free tier is genuinely unlimited after the initial 10 invoices. Unlike competitors that offer 30-day trials or freemium tiers with feature caps, Wave lets you run an entire business—invoicing, expense tracking, reporting—without paying. The catch: no advanced features, no integrations (beyond basic bank sync), and no payroll in Singapore.

If you add Stripe payment processing, you'll pay per transaction. For a Singapore consultant sending 50 invoices/month with 30% online payments, expect an additional SGD 30–50/month.

The verdict: If you're a sole trader earning under SGD 1M annually with no GST obligation and simple invoicing needs, Wave is unbeatable—free and functional. If you're GST-registered, have multiple team members, or want bank feeds from DBS/OCBC, Xero's SGD 50/month starter plan is worth the investment. Wave's free tier becomes a false economy once compliance complexity enters the picture.
